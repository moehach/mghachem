Modules

A research project investigating the bridging point between the analog and the digital.

Two Dimensions

The guiding concept of this project is the act of slicing. Before delving into the physical realm a study must first be conducted in the simpler two dimensional realm to create an understanding of what slicing entails. This sounds a lot like a quest, doesn’t it?

Lets do without the design mumbo jumbo and explain this in English: The basics of this process is to emulate the act of slicing but through digital means, What does slicing mean? Are all slices clean cuts? Or can they be rough? Can they be delicate and soft?

Three Dimensions

Now we’re talking! Does the act of slicing manifest itself in the same exact manner physically as it did physically? Or are there other factors that the static two-dimensional world does not consider? I’ll give you the answers: They’re no, and yes, respectively.

The act of slicing starts to introduces a new form of action: sliding. Models were falling apart as they were being handled. In hindsight, I will confess that I was surprised, but back then it wasn’t all that obvious. When you cut something all the way through, a piece of it will fall or be left behind since there is nothing keeping the object intact any more.

Physical but Digital

Right, now that we have our models, it's time to head right back to the digital!

The Module

Module Generation

A slice can create new forms, and a slice does not need to be a straight cut, it can operate in multiple dimensions at once. A guided approach was taken towards that resulted in the generation of a helicoidal form from one act of slicing. In English: I took a block of foam, twisted it 180°, and I found this sweet surface that I extracted, and managed to turn it into a modular unit.

The next step in the process was to take the digital back into the physical and explore various low-level methods of fabricating it.

Surface Mapping

Poster sizes have been set at 540mm x 900mm to sit cleanly on the walls with the same proportions of the bricks on the wall. Below are samples of how the template may be applied:

With the creation of a module, it was important to understand what inherent qualities it contains. The initial experimentation took the module and applied it to a surface mapping script using Grasshopper. This process allowed me to gain an insight on whether or not this extreme form of surface manipulation adds anything to the module, or if it was unnecessary to manipulate the module.

Stacking

I found surface mapping introduced too many complexities to the module, and what it brought to the table was simply not worth it. This time the module was used as a building block - Think Legos, but with more twists to it.

As soon as it was clear the modules can be stacked in all three-dimensions, I knew it was time to go back full circle to the act of slicing. Slicing here begins to act as a space-forming tool.

Atmospheric Qualities

The previous experiment revealed inherent qualities the module had that I was pleasantly surprised to have found. Depending on the exact location you slice into the module, you would be able to create spaces with varying degrees of privacy, light penetration, and still allow for effective cross-ventilation. The module was conducive to the creation of seamless transitions between a state of intimacy and agoraphobia.

Fabrication

Make it Real

The module looked promising, and itt was time to fabricate and bring it to reality one last time. Initially digital means were used but with the exception of 3D printers, it was hard if not impossible to fabricate this module using the CNC routers I had access to. So I chose to build it the good old fashioned way - With my hands, and relying solely on analogue techniques.

Ok, so the previous results aren’t very pretty - but with enough iterations I was able to finally create a plaster caster module that was faithful to the digital version of the module. The process of fabrication was relatively easy, it involved layer plaster on a foam cube that was cut in the same way the module was initially conceived. Literally just 3 steps: Cut a mold from a foam cube, layer the plaster, and seperate.
